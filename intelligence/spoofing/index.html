Spoofing &
Impersonation.

Every other abuse leans on one thing first: convincing you the sender is someone they are not. This pillar models identity forgery end to end — from lookalike domains and authentication gaps to header and brand deception. The sixth pillar of Email Abuse Intelligence, and the one your domain check measures directly.

Namespace EAI · SPO Release v1.0 Posture Defensive & analytical Techniques 24
01 · Pillar thesis

Identity is the first line — and the first thing forged.

Spoofing is the root enabler beneath spam, phishing, BEC and fraud alike. If a message can convincingly claim to be from a trusted domain or person, every lure that follows becomes easier. That is why identity is where defence pays back most — and why authentication exists.

This pillar is the direct counterpart to the tool on the home page: the domain check measures your exposure to exactly these techniques, and the SpamCrackers Standard grades the controls — SPF, DKIM and DMARC — that close them. Here the techniques are named and mapped so a grade becomes an understanding.

02 · Scope & boundaries

Defensive by definition.

What this is
  • A classification and reference model for identity forgery in email.
  • Analytical and defensive — for defenders, brand and email teams.
  • Focused on observable signals and authentication controls.
  • Open and citable, with stable identifiers.
What this is not
  • Not a tool or service for spoofing or impersonating anyone.
  • Not operational instructions for forging identity.
  • Not a directory of spoofable domains or targets.
  • Not a substitute for lawful, authorised testing.

Every technique is described at the level of classification and defence — what it is, how it is observed, and how it is countered, with authentication as the throughline.

03 · Reference Model v1.0 · SPO

A shared vocabulary for the spoofing pillar.

Three parts: a taxonomy, a tactics & techniques matrix, and a shared evidence model.

How to cite

Elements carry stable identifiers in the EAI namespace under the spoofing prefix SPO (chosen to avoid collision with the SPF authentication standard). Cite by code and URL — for example SPO-AU-03 · spamcrackers.com/intelligence/spoofing/#SPO-AU-03.

03.1 · Spoofing taxonomy

Four axes of classification.

A forgery is described by the element forged, the authentication gap used, the forgery channel and the target impersonated.

SPO-TAX-A

By forged element

CodeClassDefinition
AA1Exact domainThe real domain is forged in the visible From.
AA2Lookalike / cousinA homoglyph or typo variant of the real domain.
AA3Display nameOnly the friendly name is forged; the address is not.
AA4Subdomain / danglingA dormant subdomain or dangling record is abused.
AA5Reply-to mismatchA different reply-to quietly redirects the conversation.
AA6Homoglyph / IDNInternationalised characters imitate familiar letters.
SPO-TAX-B

By authentication gap

CodeClassDefinition
BB1No SPFNo sender-policy record to check against.
BB2Weak / no DKIMNo verifiable signature on the message.
BB3DMARC noneA policy that reports but never blocks.
BB4Misaligned authSPF or DKIM passes for a different domain than the From.
BB5Forwarder gapsLegitimate forwarding breaks authentication and is exploited.
SPO-TAX-C

By forgery channel

CodeClassDefinition
CC1Header-fromThe visible From header is spoofed.
CC2Envelope-fromThe hidden return-path is spoofed.
CC3Brand mimicryLogos, layout and tone impersonate a brand.
CC4Trust-domain abuseA genuinely trusted service or domain is misused.
CC5New-domain impersonationA fresh domain poses as an established identity.
SPO-TAX-D

By impersonated target

CodeClassDefinition
DD1BrandA company or product identity.
DD2Executive / personA named individual.
DD3Vendor / partnerA supplier or business relationship.
DD4Government / authorityA public body or regulator.
DD5Internal colleagueA coworker within the same organisation.

SPO-DP

Domain & Path Preparation

How an impersonating identity is set up before sending.

SPO-DP-01

Lookalike registration

What it is
A cousin domain is registered to resemble a trusted one (bridges SPM-ID-02).
Observable signals
Recently registered homoglyph or typo domains of a brand.
Defensive response
Lookalike and brand monitoring; defensive registrations.
SPO-DP-02

Homoglyph / IDN abuse

What it is
Internationalised or look-alike characters imitate familiar letters.
Observable signals
Non-ASCII characters imitating a known domain.
Defensive response
Homoglyph normalisation and IDN display policies.
SPO-DP-03

Subdomain & dangling abuse

What it is
A dormant subdomain or dangling DNS record is taken over to send.
Observable signals
Authenticated mail from long-inactive subdomains.
Defensive response
DNS hygiene; retire dangling records; DMARC visibility.
SPO-DP-04

New-domain impersonation

What it is
A freshly registered domain poses as an established identity.
Observable signals
Very low domain age paired with a trusted-brand pretext.
Defensive response
Newly-registered-domain scoring and greylisting.
SPO-AU

Authentication Abuse

How gaps in email authentication are exploited to forge identity.

SPO-AU-01

Missing-SPF exploitation

What it is
A domain without SPF offers no policy to fail against.
Observable signals
Forged mail from domains publishing no SPF.
Defensive response
Publish SPF ending in -all. (See the SpamCrackers Standard.)
SPO-AU-02

Missing-DKIM exploitation

What it is
Without DKIM, receivers cannot verify a signature.
Observable signals
Unsigned mail claiming a brand identity.
Defensive response
Enable DKIM signing and publish the key.
SPO-AU-03

DMARC-none exploitation

What it is
A monitoring-only policy tells receivers to block nothing.
Observable signals
Spoofed mail passing because DMARC is p=none.
Defensive response
Enforce DMARC at quarantine, then reject.
SPO-AU-04

Alignment gaps

What it is
Authentication passes for a domain other than the visible From.
Observable signals
SPF or DKIM aligned to an unrelated domain.
Defensive response
Require DMARC alignment, not just an SPF or DKIM pass.
SPO-AU-05

Forwarder-gap abuse

What it is
Legitimate forwarding that breaks SPF is exploited to slip spoofed mail.
Observable signals
Spoofed mail arriving via forwarding paths.
Defensive response
Use DKIM and ARC; account for forwarding in policy.
SPO-HD

Header & Identity Forgery

How the visible sender identity itself is faked.

SPO-HD-01

Header-from spoofing

What it is
The visible From header is forged to a trusted address.
Observable signals
From address failing authentication or alignment.
Defensive response
Enforce DMARC; flag unauthenticated From.
SPO-HD-02

Display-name impersonation

What it is
The friendly name claims a trusted identity while the address does not match.
Observable signals
A known name paired with an unfamiliar address.
Defensive response
Header analysis and external-sender warning banners.
SPO-HD-03

Reply-to redirection

What it is
A different reply-to quietly steers responses to the attacker.
Observable signals
Reply-to diverging from the visible From.
Defensive response
Flag reply-to and From mismatches.
SPO-HD-04

Envelope-from spoofing

What it is
The hidden return-path is forged independent of the visible From.
Observable signals
Return-path inconsistent with the From domain.
Defensive response
Evaluate envelope and header alignment together.
SPO-BR

Brand & Trust Mimicry

How look-and-feel is used to borrow credibility.

SPO-BR-01

Logo & layout cloning

What it is
Brand assets and layout are reproduced to appear genuine.
Observable signals
Brand assets paired with a mismatched sender.
Defensive response
Brand-to-sender consistency and logo detection.
SPO-BR-02

Trust-seal misuse

What it is
Security badges or seals are shown to imply legitimacy.
Observable signals
Trust imagery on an unauthenticated message.
Defensive response
Treat in-message seals as decoration, not proof.
SPO-BR-03

TLS-for-trust

What it is
A valid certificate on a fake domain lends a false sense of safety.
Observable signals
A padlock on a lookalike or new domain.
Defensive response
TLS is encryption, not identity; check the domain.
SPO-BR-04

Trusted-service abuse

What it is
A genuinely reputable platform is used to relay the impersonation.
Observable signals
Impersonation relayed through a trusted service.
Defensive response
Inspect content and intent, not only the relay's reputation.
SPO-DL

Delivery & Evasion

How forged mail is pushed through and kept from detection.

SPO-DL-01

Reputation borrowing

What it is
Established infrastructure or services carry the forged mail.
Observable signals
Impersonation from otherwise-reputable sources.
Defensive response
Reputation plus alignment, not reputation alone.
SPO-DL-02

Mixed-legitimacy content

What it is
Real links and text surround the forged intent to appear benign.
Observable signals
Mostly-legitimate content with a single forged element.
Defensive response
Evaluate the whole message and its authentication.
SPO-DL-03

Selective targeting

What it is
Only chosen recipients receive the forged mail to limit exposure.
Observable signals
Narrow, low-volume impersonation runs.
Defensive response
Cross-tenant intelligence sharing and DMARC reporting.
SPO-OB

Objective

What the forged identity is ultimately used to achieve.

SPO-OB-01

Phishing enablement

What it is
The forged identity makes a phishing lure credible (bridges PHI).
Observable signals
Impersonation paired with a credential-capture link.
Defensive response
Combine identity checks with URL detonation.
SPO-OB-02

BEC enablement

What it is
The forged identity underpins a payment-fraud request (bridges BEC).
Observable signals
Impersonation of an executive or vendor around payments.
Defensive response
Out-of-band verification of payment requests.
SPO-OB-03

Brand damage & disinformation

What it is
Impersonation is used to mislead or harm a brand's audience.
Observable signals
Forged communications contradicting official channels.
Defensive response
Public reporting channels and DMARC enforcement.
SPO-OB-04

Deliverability sabotage

What it is
Spoofing a domain degrades its sending reputation.
Observable signals
A rise in spoofed mail harming legitimate delivery.
Defensive response
DMARC enforcement and reputation monitoring.
03.3 · Indicators & evidence model

No claim without an observable.

The spoofing pillar shares the Email Abuse Intelligence evidence model — with authentication alignment carrying decisive weight.

CodeClassEvidence captured
EAI-EV-NETNetworkSending IP, ASN, reverse DNS and geolocation of the sender.
EAI-EV-AUTHAuthenticationSPF, DKIM and DMARC results, alignment, ARC chain — central to this pillar.
EAI-EV-CONTContentFrom, reply-to and return-path values, brand assets, header anomalies.
EAI-EV-BEHAVBehaviouralFirst-contact patterns, low-volume targeting, deviation from known senders.
EAI-EV-INFRAInfrastructureDomain age, lookalike and homoglyph registration, certificate history, registrar.
Confidence model
LowA single weak or ambiguous indicator. Suitable for enrichment, not enforcement.
ModerateMultiple independent indicators from different classes agree.
HighCorroborated indicators plus authentication-alignment evidence.
ConfirmedDirect, reproducible evidence — e.g. a From that fails aligned DMARC.
