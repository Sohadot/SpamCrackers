How to read a pattern

A chain is only as strong as its weakest step.

A pattern is not new intelligence — it is a composition of techniques already in the reference model. Reading a campaign as a chain reveals something a flat list can't: the earliest step where one control collapses everything downstream. Every technique code links to its full definition; every pattern ends at its break.

EAP-01

Exact-domain spoofing of an unauthenticated domain

SPO · Spoofing

The simplest, most preventable abuse chain: because a domain never published enforcing SPF/DMARC, anyone can send mail as it — and receivers have nothing to contradict them.

A domain publishes no SPF, or a DMARC record fixed at p=none. An operator forges the visible From address and sends from any server. Receiving systems find no enforcing policy to reject the forgery, so it can land in the inbox wearing the domain's own name — the launch pad for phishing and BEC that impersonate the brand.

The chain
  1. Opportunity SPO-AU-01 Missing-SPF exploitation No SPF record — no server list to contradict a forgery.
  2. Opportunity SPO-AU-03 DMARC-none exploitation DMARC absent or at p=none — failures are reported, never rejected.
  3. Forgery SPO-HD-01 Header-from spoofing The visible From is set to the target domain exactly.
  4. Forgery SPO-HD-02 Display-name impersonation Optionally the display name alone is impersonated for mobile clients.
  5. Payload SPO-OB-01 Phishing enablement The trusted envelope now carries a phishing lure…
  6. Payload SPO-OB-02 BEC enablement …or a business-email-compromise instruction.
Earliest break · SPO-AU-03 · DMARC p=reject

This is the one chain the SpamCrackers domain check measures directly. Publishing SPF with -all and DMARC at p=reject with alignment removes the opportunity at its root — the forgery is rejected before delivery.

Follow the remediation playbook →
Observable signals
  • From = your domain, sent from a server not in any SPF include.
  • DMARC aggregate reports show unauthenticated sources under your domain.
  • SPF result none/softfail and DMARC policy none in the received headers.
EAP-02

Adversary-in-the-middle credential phishing

PHI · Phishing

A phishing chain built to defeat multi-factor authentication by relaying the login in real time and stealing the authenticated session itself.

The target is profiled, a convincing lookalike domain is stood up with valid TLS, and an account-security pretext drives the victim to a reverse-proxy that sits between them and the real login. Credentials and the one-time code are relayed live; the stolen session cookie hands over an already-authenticated mailbox, MFA notwithstanding.

The chain
  1. Reconnaissance PHI-RT-01 OSINT profiling Public profiling identifies the person and the service they use.
  2. Infrastructure PHI-IN-01 Lookalike / cousin domains A lookalike / cousin domain is registered to host the proxy.
  3. Infrastructure PHI-IN-05 TLS-for-trust Valid TLS lends the padlock's false reassurance.
  4. Lure PHI-LP-03 Account-security pretext An account-security pretext manufactures urgency to sign in.
  5. Capture PHI-CC-02 Adversary-in-the-middle (AiTM) A reverse proxy relays the session to the genuine login in real time.
  6. Capture PHI-CC-04 OTP interception The one-time code is intercepted mid-flight.
  7. Outcome PHI-OB-01 Account takeover The stolen session cookie yields account takeover.
  8. Outcome PHI-OB-03 Mailbox persistence Mailbox rules are planted for quiet persistence.
Earliest break · PHI-CC-02 · Phishing-resistant MFA (FIDO2)

Because AiTM defeats one-time codes, the durable break is phishing-resistant authentication (hardware-bound passkeys / FIDO2), which will not authenticate to a proxy origin. Upstream, monitoring for lookalike registrations shortens the window before the lure ever sends.

Read the phishing pillar →
Observable signals
  • Login origin is a proxy/lookalike host, not the real service domain.
  • A session cookie is used from a new geography minutes after sign-in.
  • New inbox rules that auto-delete or forward security notifications.
EAP-03

Vendor-compromise invoice redirection

BEC · BEC

The highest-loss email fraud pattern: a genuine vendor mailbox is compromised and used, from inside a real thread, to redirect a legitimate payment.

Rather than impersonate, the operator takes over a supplier's actual account, reads the live correspondence to find an in-flight payment, then — inside the authentic thread, from the authentic address — announces a change of banking details just before the invoice is due. Everything authenticates, because it is really the vendor's mailbox.

The chain
  1. Reconnaissance BEC-RT-02 Relationship mapping Buyer–supplier relationships are mapped.
  2. Access BEC-AC-02 Vendor email compromise (VEC) The vendor's own mailbox is compromised (VEC).
  3. Reconnaissance BEC-RE-01 Thread-context harvesting Live threads are read for context, tone and timing.
  4. Reconnaissance BEC-RE-02 Pending-payment discovery A pending, unpaid invoice is identified.
  5. Engagement BEC-EN-04 Thread insertion The message is inserted into the genuine thread.
  6. Engagement BEC-EN-02 Vendor bank-change request A bank-detail change is requested as routine.
  7. Exploitation BEC-EX-02 Banking-detail alteration Payment routing is altered to a controlled account.
  8. Exploitation BEC-EX-01 Wire-transfer instruction The wire executes to the new details.
  9. Cash-out BEC-CO-01 Money-mule routing Funds are moved through money-mule accounts.
Earliest break · BEC-EN-02 · Out-of-band verification of banking changes

Authentication can't catch this — the mail is genuinely the vendor's. The break is procedural: any change to banking details is confirmed on a previously-known phone number, never a number supplied in the email. That single control neutralises the entire chain regardless of how convincing the thread is.

Read the BEC pillar →
Observable signals
  • Banking details change partway through an existing thread.
  • A new reply-to or subtle send-time / language shift from the vendor.
  • Pressure to pay before a stated deadline, discouraging a call-back.
EAP-04

Executive-impersonation gift-card fraud

BEC · BECSPO · Spoofing

A low-tech, high-volume BEC chain: a junior employee is pressured by a spoofed executive into buying gift cards under a veil of confidentiality.

A finance or admin junior is identified and receives a message that appears to come from a senior executive — usually only the display name is forged, from a free webmail account — asking them urgently and confidentially to buy gift cards. The confidentiality framing exists precisely to stop the victim verifying with anyone.

The chain
  1. Reconnaissance BEC-RT-01 Finance-role identification A junior finance/admin role is identified as the target.
  2. Impersonation BEC-AC-04 Display-name impersonation The executive's display name is impersonated.
  3. Engagement BEC-EN-01 Executive request An urgent executive request is issued.
  4. Engagement BEC-EN-05 Confidentiality framing Confidentiality framing isolates the victim from colleagues.
  5. Exploitation BEC-EX-04 Gift-card request Gift cards are requested and their codes exfiltrated.
  6. Exploitation BEC-EX-05 Verification suppression Verification is actively suppressed ('I'm in a meeting').
Earliest break · BEC-EX-05 · A 'no gift cards, always verify' norm

Enforcing DMARC and display-name / external-sender banners strips the disguise, and a standing rule — leadership never requests gift cards by email, and any such request is verified in person — makes the verification-suppression step fail. The chain depends on the victim not checking; remove that and it collapses.

Read the BEC pillar →
Observable signals
  • Executive display name over a free-webmail or lookalike address.
  • Urgency plus secrecy: 'don't tell anyone, I'm unavailable to call'.
  • A request for gift-card purchases or their redemption codes.
EAP-05

Macro-document loader to ransomware

MAL · Malware

The classic malware-delivery chain: a lure document runs a macro that pulls a loader, which stages the final ransomware payload.

A macro-enabled document arrives as an attachment, often inside a password-protected archive with the password in the body to defeat scanning. If the recipient enables content, the macro fetches and runs a loader, which persists and stages the payload — frequently ransomware.

The chain
  1. Packaging MAL-PK-02 Macro-enabled document A macro-enabled Office document is prepared as the lure.
  2. Delivery MAL-DL-01 Attachment delivery It is delivered as an email attachment.
  3. Evasion MAL-EV-03 Password gating Password gating hides the payload from gateway scanners.
  4. Evasion MAL-EV-01 Sandbox evasion Sandbox checks delay execution in analysis environments.
  5. Execution MAL-EX-01 Macro execution Enabling content runs the macro.
  6. Install MAL-IN-01 Dropper / loader stage A dropper / loader stage fetches the next payload.
  7. Install MAL-IN-02 Autostart persistence Autostart entries establish persistence.
  8. Impact MAL-OB-02 Ransomware impact Ransomware encrypts and extorts.
Earliest break · MAL-EX-01 · Block macros from the internet; detonate attachments

Disabling macros in files that arrive from the internet by policy prevents the execution step outright. Detonating attachments in a sandbox and stripping active content at the gateway remove the delivery advantage the password-gating was designed to buy.

Read the malware pillar →
Observable signals
  • A password-protected archive with the password written in the email body.
  • A document that insists you 'Enable Editing / Enable Content'.
  • Outbound fetch to an unfamiliar host immediately after opening a document.
EAP-06

Reply-chain hijack malware delivery

MAL · MalwarePHI · Phishing

Malware delivered from a real, compromised account into a genuine existing thread — trust is borrowed from the conversation, so authentication alone won't stop it.

An attacker with access to a legitimate mailbox replies into a real, ongoing thread, attaching a payload package. Because the message authenticates and continues a conversation the recipient recognises, the usual sender-trust cues all point the wrong way; the defence has to move to the content.

The chain
  1. Access PHI-IN-06 Compromised sender account A legitimate sender account is compromised.
  2. Delivery MAL-DL-04 Reply-chain delivery The payload is sent as a reply within a real thread.
  3. Packaging MAL-PK-04 HTML smuggling package HTML smuggling assembles the file in the browser to evade scanning.
  4. Execution MAL-EX-04 User-run persuasion Familiar context persuades the user to run it.
  5. Outcome MAL-OB-03 Remote control The operator gains remote control of the host.
Earliest break · MAL-PK-04 · Content detonation, not sender trust

Because the sender is genuine and the thread is real, DMARC and reputation pass — so the break must be at the content: detonate attachments and reconstruct smuggled files server-side, and flag unexpected archives or HTML attachments even from known correspondents.

Read the malware pillar →
Observable signals
  • A reply into a genuine old thread that suddenly carries an attachment.
  • An HTML attachment that assembles a downloadable file locally.
  • An archive or macro document unexpected from a known contact.
EAP-07

Snowshoe credential-harvest spam

SPM · SpamPHI · Phishing

Volume abuse that spreads a campaign thinly across many low-reputation sources to stay under per-IP thresholds while funnelling victims to a credential-harvest page.

Sending is distributed across a wide fan of low-volume IPs and freshly-warmed domains so no single source trips a volumetric filter. Urgent, brand-flavoured lures push recipients through cloaked redirects to a harvest page; captured credentials are then resold.

The chain
  1. Infrastructure SPM-IA-01 Snowshoe distribution Snowshoe distribution spreads volume across many /24s.
  2. Infrastructure SPM-DE-02 Reputation warming Reputation warming primes fresh sending identities.
  3. Lure SPM-CL-01 Urgency & scarcity framing Urgency & scarcity framing drives the click.
  4. Lure SPM-CL-02 Brand & authority impersonation Brand & authority impersonation borrows trust.
  5. Delivery SPM-DE-01 Redirect & cloaking chains Redirect & cloaking chains hide the true destination.
  6. Capture SPM-EP-01 Credential-harvest destinations A credential-harvest destination collects logins.
  7. Monetisation SPM-OBJ-04 Credential & data resale Harvested credentials & data are resold.
Earliest break · SPM-IA-01 · Reputation + redirect analysis at scale

Per-IP thresholds are exactly what snowshoe defeats, so the break is aggregate: cross-source reputation and campaign clustering catch the fan of thin senders, while unwrapping redirect chains exposes the shared harvest destination the lures all point to.

Read the spam pillar →
Observable signals
  • Many low-reputation sources each sending tiny volumes of similar mail.
  • Freshly-registered or recently-warmed sending domains.
  • Links that pass through cloaking redirects to a lookalike login page.
EAP-08

Advance-fee & romance fraud

SCM · Scam

A long-game social chain that builds rapport, moves the victim off-platform, then escalates a series of fees against a manufactured payoff that never arrives.

An opener is broadcast or narrowly targeted; a responder is drawn into a relationship or opportunity, migrated to a private channel, and slowly committed through small reciprocity. Fees then escalate against sunk cost until the victim can no longer justify stopping — paid over irreversible rails.

The chain
  1. Casting SCM-LB-01 Mass blast A mass blast seeds a wide pool of potential victims.
  2. Hook SCM-HK-02 Romance opener A romance (or windfall/investment) opener engages a responder.
  3. Trust SCM-TR-03 Small reciprocity Small reciprocity manufactures a sense of obligation.
  4. Trust SCM-TR-01 Channel migration The victim is migrated to a private channel, off oversight.
  5. Escalation SCM-ES-01 Rising fees Rising fees are introduced one after another.
  6. Escalation SCM-ES-02 Sunk-cost pressure Sunk-cost pressure keeps the victim paying.
  7. Exploitation SCM-EX-01 Advance-fee demand An advance-fee demand extracts the first payment…
  8. Exploitation SCM-EX-03 Crypto transfer …over irreversible crypto rails.
  9. Cash-out SCM-CO-01 Mule networks Mule networks launder the proceeds.
Earliest break · SCM-TR-01 · The upfront-fee tell

The reliable break is a rule, not a filter: a legitimate prize, employer or partner never requires an upfront fee or crypto payment to release money. Channel migration off a monitored platform is the earliest observable tell — treat it as the moment to disengage and verify.

Read the scam pillar →
Observable signals
  • A quick push to move to WhatsApp/Telegram or private email.
  • Escalating fees, taxes or 'release charges' against a promised payoff.
  • Payment demanded in gift cards or cryptocurrency.
Method & honesty

What a pattern is — and isn't.

These archetypes are analytical compositions, not incident reports. They describe how well-documented classes of email abuse are structured, so defenders can reason about where to intervene. They are deliberately not attributed to any named actor, and they contain no operational detail — every step is a link to a classification-and-defence technique, nothing more.

Each pattern is built only from techniques that exist in the published model, and the build fails if a reference doesn't resolve — so a pattern can never drift from the reference beneath it. The set is versioned as Patterns Library v1.0 alongside Reference Model v1.0; the underlying technique identifiers are unchanged. The library is published as machine-readable JSON under CC BY 4.0.
