01 · Category thesis

Detection is a feature. Intelligence is an asset.

The market is saturated with tools that answer one shallow question — is this message spam? — and then forget everything the moment the verdict is rendered. Each product re-learns the same adversary in private, names techniques differently, and shares nothing. There is no common reference for how email abuse actually works.

That gap is the opportunity. Email Abuse Intelligence is not another filter. It is the connective layer above detection: a durable, citable body of knowledge that classifies the adversary's infrastructure, identity tactics, lures, evasion and objectives — and the evidence that reveals them. Where a filter produces a verdict, an intelligence layer produces understanding that compounds over time.

The abuse landscape is wider than spam alone — it spans phishing, business email compromise, scams, malware delivery and spoofing. So the reference is built as an umbrella from day one, and detailed one pillar at a time. This release opens with spam because it is the highest-volume, most-observable surface — the ideal foundation for a shared model.

02 · Scope & boundaries

Defensive by definition.

What this is
  • A classification and reference model for understanding email-abuse campaigns.
  • Analytical and defensive — written for defenders, responders and researchers.
  • Focused on observable signals and countermeasures, so abuse can be detected and stopped.
  • Open and citable, with stable identifiers others can reference.
What this is not
  • Not a tool, service or infrastructure for sending spam or running campaigns.
  • Not operational instructions — no step-by-step methods for conducting abuse.
  • Not a directory of targets, victims or exploitable weaknesses to abuse.
  • Not a substitute for lawful, authorised security testing and consent.

Every technique below is described at the level of classification and defence — what it is, how it is observed, and how it is countered — in the tradition of open defensive frameworks. Where a technique bridges into targeted fraud, it points to the relevant pillar rather than elaborating an attack.

SPM-IA

Infrastructure Acquisition

How senders obtain the machines, addresses and networks to send from.

SPM-IA-01

Snowshoe distribution

What it is
Sending volume is spread thinly across a wide range of IP addresses and domains so no single source crosses a reputation or rate threshold.
Observable signals
Many low-volume, newly-seen IPs across unrelated netblocks; shared content fingerprints across sources.
Defensive response
Score reputation at the netblock and ASN level, not just per-IP; baseline expected volume per source.
In patternsEAP-07
SPM-IA-02

Botnet / compromised hosts

What it is
Mail originates from large numbers of compromised end-user machines.
Observable signals
Residential or dynamic IP ranges, missing or generic reverse DNS, inconsistent HELO identities.
Defensive response
Apply policy-block and dynamic-user lists; treat residential-origin mail with caution.
SPM-IA-03

Compromised account abuse

What it is
A legitimate mailbox or tenant is taken over and used to send.
Observable signals
Sudden change in sending behaviour, geolocation or velocity anomalies, unusual send times.
Defensive response
Authentication-anomaly detection, adaptive rate limits, session and device baselining.
SPM-IA-04

ESP / bulk-provider abuse

What it is
Legitimate email-service providers are used to distribute abusive lists.
Observable signals
Valid provider headers paired with abusive content or unconsented recipients.
Defensive response
Honour feedback loops, track reputation per campaign and per sub-account, complaint-rate monitoring.
SPM-IA-05

Hijacked infrastructure

What it is
Dormant subdomains, dangling DNS records or routing weaknesses are abused to send authenticated-looking mail.
Observable signals
Authenticated mail from long-inactive subdomains; records pointing at unclaimed resources.
Defensive response
Routine DNS hygiene, retire dangling records, use DMARC reporting for visibility.
SPM-ID

Identity & Authentication Abuse

How campaigns forge, borrow or dodge sender identity and authentication.

SPM-ID-01

Exact-domain spoofing

What it is
A real domain is forged in the From header where authentication is absent or unenforced.
Observable signals
SPF or DKIM failure with a DMARC policy of none; envelope and header mismatch.
Defensive response
Enforce DMARC at quarantine then reject; align SPF and DKIM. (See the SpamCrackers Standard.)
SPM-ID-02

Lookalike / cousin domains

What it is
Domains that visually or typographically resemble a trusted brand.
Observable signals
Recently registered homoglyph or typo variants; low domain age; brand terms in the label.
Defensive response
Brand and lookalike monitoring, homoglyph normalisation, newly-registered-domain scoring.
SPM-ID-03

Display-name impersonation

What it is
The friendly display name claims a trusted identity while the real address does not match.
Observable signals
Friendly-From to envelope-From mismatch; free-mail address behind a corporate display name.
Defensive response
Header-From analysis, first-contact and external-sender warning banners.
SPM-ID-04

Newly-registered-domain sending

What it is
Domains registered shortly before a campaign, with no reputation to lose.
Observable signals
Domain age below a small threshold; registrar and nameserver clustering.
Defensive response
Age-based scoring and short-lived greylisting for very new domains.
SPM-ID-05

Authentication-passing abuse

What it is
Mail that legitimately passes SPF/DKIM/DMARC yet is still abusive.
Observable signals
Aligned authentication combined with abusive content, lists or behaviour.
Defensive response
Reputation and content analysis beyond authentication; authentication proves identity, not intent.
SPM-CL

Content & Lure Construction

How the message body is built to persuade and to evade analysis.

SPM-CL-01

Urgency & scarcity framing

What it is
Language engineered to pressure fast, unconsidered action.
Observable signals
Deadline, suspension and reward markers; high imperative density.
Defensive response
Linguistic lure classifiers; combine with sender reputation rather than keywords alone.
In patternsEAP-07
SPM-CL-02

Brand & authority impersonation

What it is
Trusted logos, layouts and tone used to borrow credibility.
Observable signals
Brand assets paired with a mismatched or unauthenticated sender.
Defensive response
Brand-asset matching, logo detection, sender-to-brand consistency checks.
In patternsEAP-07
SPM-CL-03

Obfuscation

What it is
Intent is hidden from filters using encoding, invisible characters or image-only bodies.
Observable signals
High image-to-text ratio, unicode anomalies, zero-width characters.
Defensive response
Unicode normalisation, optical character recognition, structural analysis.
SPM-CL-04

Content polymorphism

What it is
Each recipient receives a slightly different message to defeat exact signatures.
Observable signals
High template entropy; weak exact-match clustering across a burst.
Defensive response
Fuzzy hashing and near-duplicate clustering rather than exact signatures.
SPM-DE

Delivery & Evasion

How campaigns reach inboxes while avoiding detection and takedown.

SPM-DE-01

Redirect & cloaking chains

What it is
Links pass through shorteners and multiple hops, sometimes serving different content to analysers than to victims.
Observable signals
URL shorteners, multi-hop redirects, geo- or user-agent-based cloaking.
Defensive response
URL detonation in a sandbox, follow chains, analyse final destinations.
In patternsEAP-07
SPM-DE-02

Reputation warming

What it is
A new IP or domain is ramped up gradually to build trust before abuse.
Observable signals
Steady volume increase on young infrastructure; benign early sends.
Defensive response
Model sender tenure and velocity together; treat trust as earned over time.
In patternsEAP-07
SPM-DE-03

Send-and-burn

What it is
Infrastructure is used intensively for a short window then abandoned.
Observable signals
Sources active for hours then silent; rapid infrastructure turnover.
Defensive response
Fast indicator sharing, first-contact caution, short-tenure penalties.
SPM-DE-04

List bombing & subscription abuse

What it is
Signup and confirmation flows are abused to flood a target with mail.
Observable signals
Bursts of subscription-confirmation messages to one recipient.
Defensive response
Rate limits and challenge mechanisms on signup forms; double-opt-in hygiene.
SPM-DE-05

Locale & timing targeting

What it is
Delivery is timed to a victim's business hours and locale to raise engagement.
Observable signals
Send timing aligned to recipient time zone and working hours.
Defensive response
Temporal baselining of normal correspondence patterns.
SPM-EP

Engagement & Payload

How recipients are converted into clicks, credentials or infections.

SPM-EP-01

Credential-harvest destinations

What it is
Cloned login pages hosted to capture entered credentials.
Observable signals
Login clones on newly-registered domains or throwaway hosting; brand mismatch.
Defensive response
Destination reputation, sandbox rendering, credential-form detection.
In patternsEAP-07
SPM-EP-02

Weaponised attachments

What it is
Documents or archives carrying macros, scripts or executables.
Observable signals
Macro-enabled documents, risky archive and file types, double extensions.
Defensive response
Attachment sandboxing, file-type policy, macro restrictions.
SPM-EP-03

Reply funnels

What it is
The message invites a reply that moves the victim into a fraud conversation.
Observable signals
Free-mail reply-to, thread hijacking, reply-to and From divergence.
Defensive response
Reply-to and From mismatch flags, conversation-thread integrity checks.
SPM-EP-04

Tracking beacons

What it is
Invisible images and unique links confirm live, engaged recipients.
Observable signals
One-pixel images, per-recipient tracking URLs.
Defensive response
Image proxying and stripping, link rewriting and neutralisation.
SPM-OBJ

Monetisation & Objective

The end goals a campaign is built to achieve.

SPM-OBJ-01

Affiliate & lead resale

What it is
Clicks and leads are monetised through commission networks.
Observable signals
Affiliate tracking parameters, redirection to marketing funnels.
Defensive response
Destination and network reputation, funnel fingerprinting.
SPM-OBJ-02

Advance-fee & romance fraud

What it is
Long-form social engineering aimed at direct payment.
Observable signals
Emotional narratives, requests to move to private channels.
Defensive response
Narrative classifiers, reply-path analysis, user education.
SPM-OBJ-03

Invoice & payment fraud

What it is
Fake or altered invoices and payment-redirection requests (bridges to the BEC pillar).
Observable signals
Payment-detail changes, urgency around transfers, vendor impersonation.
Defensive response
Out-of-band verification of payment changes, vendor allow-lists.
SPM-OBJ-04

Credential & data resale

What it is
Harvested credentials and data are sold or reused downstream.
Observable signals
Post-compromise access from harvesting campaigns.
Defensive response
Credential-reuse monitoring, forced resets, breach correlation.
In patternsEAP-07
SPM-OBJ-05

Extortion

What it is
Threats and coercion, including mass sextortion, demanding payment.
Observable signals
Threatening templates, cryptocurrency payment demands, mass identical sends.
Defensive response
Template clustering, payment-indicator extraction, awareness messaging.
04.3 · Indicators & evidence model

No claim without an observable.

Techniques are hypotheses until evidence supports them. This model organises the observables that substantiate a classification, and how they combine into confidence.

CodeClassEvidence captured
EAI-EV-NETNetworkSending IP, ASN, reverse DNS, netblock and geolocation, HELO/EHLO identity.
EAI-EV-AUTHAuthenticationSPF result, DKIM result and selector, DMARC policy and alignment, ARC chain.
EAI-EV-CONTContentURL and domain patterns, file and body hashes, template and layout fingerprints, header anomalies.
EAI-EV-BEHAVBehaviouralVolume and velocity, send timing, list-bombing bursts, engagement tracking.
EAI-EV-INFRAInfrastructureDomain age and registrar, nameservers, hosting, redirect chains, certificate history.
Confidence model
LowA single weak or ambiguous indicator. Suitable for enrichment, not enforcement.
ModerateMultiple independent indicators from different classes point the same way.
HighCorroborated indicators plus authentication or infrastructure evidence.
ConfirmedDirect, reproducible evidence — e.g. a detonated payload or an observed campaign artefact.

Each class and tier carries a stable identifier and a precise combination rule — with an honest note on which class this site actually observes — in the full Evidence & Confidence Model.
