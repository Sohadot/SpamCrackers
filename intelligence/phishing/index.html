Email Abuse Intelligence · Pillar PHI

Phishing Intelligence.

How credential-theft campaigns are built — reconnaissance, identity, lure, delivery, capture and objective — described defensively so they can be recognised, evidenced and stopped. The second pillar of Email Abuse Intelligence.

Namespace EAI · PHI Release v1.0 Posture Defensive & analytical Techniques 30
01 · Pillar thesis

Phishing is not a message. It is a supply chain.

A phishing email is only the visible tip. Behind it sits a chain: targets are researched, infrastructure and identity are assembled, a pretext is crafted, delivery evades analysis, credentials or sessions are captured, and access is finally exploited. Treating phishing as a single “bad email” misses every point at which it can be seen and broken.

This pillar models that chain the same way the spam pillar models bulk abuse — a shared vocabulary of tactics, techniques and evidence, each citable by a stable identifier. It captures how the modern adversary defeats defences that once worked, including real-time adversary-in-the-middle proxies and OAuth consent phishing that never asks for a password.

02 · Scope & boundaries

Defensive by definition.

What this is
  • A classification and reference model for understanding phishing campaigns.
  • Analytical and defensive — written for defenders, responders and researchers.
  • Focused on observable signals and countermeasures.
  • Open and citable, with stable identifiers.
What this is not
  • Not a phishing kit, template or infrastructure of any kind.
  • Not operational instructions for conducting phishing.
  • Not a directory of targets or exploitable victims.
  • Not a substitute for lawful, authorised, consented testing.

Every technique is described at the level of classification and defence — what it is, how it is observed, and how it is countered — in the tradition of open defensive frameworks. Techniques that lead into fraud point to the relevant pillar rather than elaborating an attack.

03 · Reference Model v1.0 · PHI

A shared vocabulary for the phishing pillar.

Three parts: a taxonomy that classifies campaigns, a tactics & techniques matrix that describes how they operate, and a shared evidence model.

How to cite

Elements carry stable identifiers in the EAI namespace under the phishing prefix PHI. Cite by code and URL — for example PHI-CC-02 · spamcrackers.com/intelligence/phishing/#PHI-CC-02.

PHI-RT

Reconnaissance & Targeting

How targets, pretexts and timing are chosen before a single message is sent.

PHI-RT-01

OSINT profiling

What it is
Public information is gathered to select and tailor a convincing approach to a specific person or team.
Observable signals
Lures referencing niche, publicly-available detail about the recipient or their role.
Defensive response
Reduce unnecessary public exposure of staff detail; brief high-risk roles.
PHI-RT-02

Address harvesting

What it is
Valid recipient addresses are collected from leaks, scraping or guessing patterns.
Observable signals
Delivery to scraped, generated or rarely-used addresses.
Defensive response
Address aliasing, breach-exposure monitoring, catch-all hygiene.
PHI-RT-03

Org-structure mapping

What it is
Reporting lines and relationships are mapped to make impersonation credible.
Observable signals
Impersonation of a specific internal role or a known supplier contact.
Defensive response
Establish internal verification norms for unusual requests.
PHI-RT-04

Event-timed targeting

What it is
Campaigns are timed to payroll runs, tax season, outages or corporate events.
Observable signals
A lure aligned suspiciously well to a current internal or public event.
Defensive response
Heighten scrutiny during known high-risk windows; pre-warn staff.
PHI-IN

Infrastructure & Identity

How senders build credible-looking hosting and sender identity.

PHI-IN-01

Lookalike / cousin domains

What it is
Domains that resemble a trusted brand by homoglyph or typo (bridges SPM-ID-02).
Observable signals
Recently registered similar domains; brand terms in the label.
Defensive response
Brand and lookalike monitoring, homoglyph normalisation.
PHI-IN-02

Compromised legitimate hosting

What it is
Landing pages are hosted on hacked but reputable websites to inherit their trust.
Observable signals
A login clone on an unrelated, otherwise-reputable domain path.
Defensive response
Destination reputation and content inspection, not domain trust alone.
PHI-IN-03

Newly-registered domains

What it is
Domains registered shortly before use, with no reputation to lose.
Observable signals
Very low domain age; registrar and nameserver clustering.
Defensive response
Age-based scoring and short-lived greylisting for new domains.
PHI-IN-04

Free hosting & redirectors

What it is
Legitimate free platforms and open redirectors are abused to host or forward to the payload.
Observable signals
Final destination on free-host subdomains or via open redirects.
Defensive response
Expand and inspect full redirect chains to the true endpoint.
PHI-IN-05

TLS-for-trust

What it is
A valid certificate is obtained so the destination shows a padlock and looks legitimate.
Observable signals
A brand-new certificate on a lookalike or throwaway domain.
Defensive response
Treat TLS as encryption, not identity; monitor certificate transparency.
PHI-IN-06

Compromised sender account

What it is
Messages are sent from a real, trusted mailbox that has been taken over (bridges SPM-IA-03).
Observable signals
Behavioural anomalies on an otherwise-trusted internal or partner account.
Defensive response
Authentication-anomaly detection, adaptive rate limits.
PHI-LP

Lure & Pretext

The story engineered to earn a click or a reply.

PHI-LP-01

Brand impersonation

What it is
Trusted brand identity, layout and tone are cloned to borrow credibility.
Observable signals
Brand assets paired with a mismatched or unauthenticated sender.
Defensive response
Brand-to-sender consistency checks, logo detection.
PHI-LP-02

Authority & urgency

What it is
Pressure from a claimed authority pushes fast, unconsidered action.
Observable signals
Deadline, suspension or executive-tone markers with an external link.
Defensive response
Verify unusual requests through a separate, trusted channel.
PHI-LP-03

Account-security pretext

What it is
A message claims the account is at risk and must be “verified” now.
Observable signals
Security-alert templates driving to an external sign-in link.
Defensive response
Never authenticate through emailed links; navigate directly.
PHI-LP-04

Payment & invoice pretext

What it is
Fake invoices, receipts or payment-detail changes (bridges the BEC pillar).
Observable signals
Invoice or banking-change requests, especially with urgency.
Defensive response
Out-of-band verification of any payment change.
PHI-LP-05

Reply-chain hijacking

What it is
The lure is inserted into a genuine, existing email thread to inherit its trust.
Observable signals
An unexpected reply resuming an old thread with a new link or file.
Defensive response
Scrutinise links and attachments even within known threads.
PHI-DE

Delivery & Evasion

How the lure reaches the inbox and evades automated analysis.

PHI-DE-01

HTML smuggling

What it is
The payload is assembled inside the browser from a benign-looking HTML file to bypass gateway scanning.
Observable signals
HTML attachments that build content or downloads client-side.
Defensive response
Sandbox rendering, restrict risky attachment types.
PHI-DE-02

QR-code phishing (quishing)

What it is
The link is delivered as a QR image, pushing the victim onto a less-protected personal device.
Observable signals
An image-heavy message with a QR code and little text.
Defensive response
Extract and analyse URLs from QR images; brief staff on the tactic.
PHI-DE-03

Redirect & cloaking chains

What it is
Multi-hop redirection serves different content to analysers than to victims (shared with SPM-DE-01).
Observable signals
Shorteners, multi-hop redirects, geo- or user-agent-based cloaking.
Defensive response
Detonate URLs and follow chains from multiple vantage points.
PHI-DE-04

Link obfuscation

What it is
The true destination is hidden behind encoding or lookalike URLs.
Observable signals
Encoded URLs, or visible text that disagrees with the underlying link.
Defensive response
URL normalisation, expose the real href before allowing the click.
PHI-DE-05

Sandbox evasion

What it is
CAPTCHA, geo or time gating makes analysers see benign content while victims see the lure.
Observable signals
Content that differs between an automated analyser and a real user.
Defensive response
Realistic detonation environments and repeat analysis over time.
PHI-CC

Credential Capture

How credentials, tokens and sessions are actually stolen.

PHI-CC-01

Cloned login page

What it is
A pixel-accurate fake sign-in page records whatever the victim enters.
Observable signals
A credential form served from a non-official domain.
Defensive response
Password managers withhold autofill off-domain; verify the domain.
PHI-CC-02

Adversary-in-the-middle (AiTM)

What it is
A live reverse proxy relays to the real site, capturing the authenticated session and defeating many MFA methods.
Observable signals
Reverse-proxy patterns and real-time token relay.
Defensive response
Phishing-resistant MFA (FIDO2/passkeys), token binding, conditional access.
PHI-CC-03

OAuth consent phishing

What it is
The victim is tricked into granting an application permission — no password is ever needed.
Observable signals
Consent prompts from unverified or over-scoped applications.
Defensive response
Application governance, restrict user consent, review granted scopes.
PHI-CC-04

OTP interception

What it is
The victim is prompted to relay a one-time code in real time so the attacker can complete sign-in.
Observable signals
A page requesting an OTP immediately after a password.
Defensive response
Phishing-resistant MFA; educate users never to relay codes.
PHI-CC-05

Progressive data harvesting

What it is
Staged forms collect more than credentials — personal, financial or recovery data.
Observable signals
Multi-step forms requesting escalating personal information.
Defensive response
Detect multi-step credential-and-data form flows.
PHI-OB

Exploitation & Objective

What the stolen access is ultimately used for.

PHI-OB-01

Account takeover

What it is
Captured credentials or sessions are used to seize the account.
Observable signals
Anomalous logins shortly after a campaign.
Defensive response
Session revocation, conditional access, impossible-travel detection.
PHI-OB-02

BEC pivot

What it is
A trusted mailbox is used to run financial fraud from the inside (bridges the BEC pillar).
Observable signals
Internal payment or vendor requests from a genuine account.
Defensive response
Out-of-band verification of payment and vendor changes.
PHI-OB-03

Mailbox persistence

What it is
Forwarding rules and OAuth tokens are planted for durable, quiet access.
Observable signals
New auto-forwarding rules or unfamiliar OAuth grants.
Defensive response
Audit inbox rules and tokens, alert on new grants.
PHI-OB-04

Data exfiltration

What it is
Mail and files are harvested from the compromised account.
Observable signals
Bulk access or download activity after compromise.
Defensive response
Data-loss prevention and access-anomaly detection.
PHI-OB-05

Onward propagation

What it is
The victim's own contacts are phished from within the trusted account.
Observable signals
Internal phishing originating from a trusted mailbox.
Defensive response
Internal-phish detection and rapid account containment.
03.3 · Indicators & evidence model

No claim without an observable.

The phishing pillar shares the Email Abuse Intelligence evidence model: the observable classes that substantiate a classification, and how they combine into confidence.

CodeClassEvidence captured
EAI-EV-NETNetworkSending IP, ASN, reverse DNS, netblock and geolocation.
EAI-EV-AUTHAuthenticationSPF, DKIM and DMARC results and alignment for the sending identity.
EAI-EV-CONTContentURL and domain patterns, cloned-page and template fingerprints, header anomalies.
EAI-EV-BEHAVBehaviouralLogin anomalies, consent grants, new inbox rules, engagement tracking.
EAI-EV-INFRAInfrastructureDomain age, certificate history, hosting, redirect chains, registrar.
Confidence model
LowA single weak or ambiguous indicator. Suitable for enrichment, not enforcement.
ModerateMultiple independent indicators from different classes agree.
HighCorroborated indicators plus authentication or infrastructure evidence.
ConfirmedDirect, reproducible evidence — e.g. a detonated credential-capture page.
