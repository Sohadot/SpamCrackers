Glossary.

The vocabulary of email abuse and authentication — 40 terms, defined plainly. Every entry links to how the concept is exploited and how to defend against it, so a definition is only ever one click from action.

Advance-fee fraud

419 scam

A scam promising a large payout that is only released after the victim pays a series of upfront fees.

Technique ↗

AiTM

Adversary-in-the-Middle

A phishing technique using a live reverse proxy that relays to the real site, capturing the authenticated session and defeating many forms of MFA.

Technique ↗

ARC

Authenticated Received Chain

A standard that preserves authentication results across intermediaries such as mailing lists and forwarders, so legitimate mail is not broken by forwarding.

Forwarder abuse ↗

BEC

Business Email Compromise

Targeted fraud in which an attacker impersonates a trusted party — an executive or vendor — to redirect a payment. It carries no malware, only a plausible request.

Pillar ↗

BIMI

Brand Indicators for Message Identification

A standard that lets a domain publish a logo shown next to authenticated mail. It requires an enforced DMARC policy, rewarding strong authentication with brand visibility.

DMARC ↗

Botnet

A network of compromised machines used to send abuse at scale from many residential addresses.

Technique ↗

Compromised account

Account takeover

A legitimate mailbox taken over by an attacker and used to send trusted-looking abuse.

Technique ↗

Display-name impersonation

Friendly-from spoofing

Forging only the friendly display name of a sender while the actual address does not match — effective because many clients hide the real address.

Technique ↗

DKIM

DomainKeys Identified Mail

An email authentication method that attaches a cryptographic signature to each message. Receivers verify it with a public key published in DNS, proving the message is genuine and unaltered.

Fix it ↗Abuse ↗

DKIM selector

A label that identifies which published key verifies a given DKIM signature, allowing multiple and rotating keys per domain.

Fix it ↗

DMARC

Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting and Conformance

A policy that ties SPF and DKIM to the visible From address and tells receivers what to do on failure — monitor, quarantine or reject. The only control that reliably stops exact-domain spoofing.

Fix it ↗Abuse ↗

DMARC alignment

adkim, aspf

The requirement that the domain which passes SPF or DKIM matches the visible From domain. Without alignment, a message can pass authentication for an unrelated domain yet still spoof yours.

Technique ↗

DMARC policy

p=none, p=quarantine, p=reject

The enforcement level a domain requests. none only monitors, quarantine sends failures to spam, and reject blocks them outright.

Rollout ↗

DMARC reporting

rua, ruf

Addresses in a DMARC record where receivers send aggregate (rua) and forensic (ruf) reports, revealing who is sending as your domain.

Fix it ↗

DoH

DNS over HTTPS

DNS queries carried over an encrypted HTTPS connection. SpamCrackers uses DoH to read a domain's public records directly in your browser.

The tool ↗

Envelope-from

Return-Path, MAIL FROM

The hidden sender address used during mail transfer and checked by SPF — distinct from the visible From header.

Header forgery ↗

Feedback loop

FBL

An arrangement where a mailbox provider forwards spam complaints back to a sender, so abuse and list problems can be detected and stopped.

ESP abuse ↗

Header-from

From header

The visible sender address a recipient sees. DMARC aligns authentication to this address; spoofing it is the core of impersonation.

Technique ↗

Homoglyph

IDN homograph

A character that visually resembles another — often from a different alphabet — used to build domains that look identical to a legitimate one.

Technique ↗

HTML smuggling

A delivery technique where a benign-looking HTML attachment assembles the payload inside the browser, bypassing gateway scanning.

Malware ↗Phishing ↗

Loader

Dropper

A small first-stage program that installs and launches further malware once it runs.

Technique ↗

Lookalike domain

Cousin domain

A domain registered to closely resemble a trusted one, using typos or swapped characters, to impersonate it convincingly.

Technique ↗

Malspam

Malware spam

Unsolicited mail whose purpose is to deliver a malicious payload rather than to advertise.

Pillar ↗

Money mule

A person recruited, often unknowingly, to receive and forward stolen funds, breaking the money trail for fraudsters.

Technique ↗

MX record

Mail Exchange

A DNS record that directs a domain's inbound mail to the servers that receive it. Without an MX record a domain cannot receive email.

Fix it ↗

Newly-registered domain

NRD

A domain registered very recently, with no reputation history. A common signal of throwaway abuse infrastructure.

Technique ↗

OAuth consent phishing

Illicit consent grant

Tricking a user into granting a malicious application permission to their account — no password is ever needed, and MFA does not help.

Technique ↗

Open redirect

A legitimate site's URL that forwards to an arbitrary destination, abused to lend trust to a link that ends at a malicious page.

Delivery ↗

Phishing

Deceptive messages that trick recipients into revealing credentials or data, usually via a cloned destination.

Pillar ↗

Quishing

QR phishing

Phishing that delivers the malicious link as a QR code image, pushing the victim onto a less-protected personal device.

Technique ↗

Reply-to

A header that sets where replies are sent. A mismatch with the From address can quietly redirect a conversation to an attacker.

Technique ↗

Reputation warming

Gradually ramping volume on a new IP or domain with benign mail to build sender trust before abusing it.

Technique ↗

Snowshoe spamming

Spreading spam thinly across many IP addresses and domains so no single source crosses a reputation or rate threshold.

Technique ↗

Spear phishing

Phishing tailored to a specific individual using researched detail, far harder to spot than mass phishing.

Taxonomy ↗

SPF

Sender Policy Framework

A DNS TXT record listing which mail servers are authorised to send for a domain. Receivers check it against the connecting server; a record ending in -all asks them to reject everything else.

Fix it ↗Abuse ↗

SPF flattening

Replacing nested SPF include: mechanisms with their resolved IP addresses to stay within the 10-lookup limit.

Fix it ↗

SPF qualifier

-all, ~all, ?all

The final mechanism of an SPF record. -all is a hard fail (reject), ~all a soft fail (accept but mark), and ?all neutral (no opinion).

Fix it ↗

Spoofing

Email spoofing

Forging the sender identity of a message so it appears to come from a trusted domain or person. The root enabler beneath most other email abuse.

Pillar ↗

VEC

Vendor Email Compromise

A form of BEC where a supplier's real mailbox is compromised and used to defraud its customers, often via a banking-detail change.

Technique ↗

Whaling

Phishing aimed at executives and other high-value decision-makers.
