Playbook.

A grade tells you where you stand. This tells you what to do. Close every gap in the SpamCrackers Standard — SPF, DKIM, DMARC and MX — with exact steps, example records, and the abuse each control shuts down. Then recheck.

The four controls

Four records. One armored domain.

Each control is worth 25 points under the Standard. Fix them in order and an EXPOSED domain becomes ARMORED.

SPF

Sender Policy Framework

+25 pts

Declares which servers may send mail as your domain. Without it, anyone can send as you and nothing contradicts them.

Remediation
  1. List every service that sends mail for you — your mail platform, marketing tool, CRM, ticketing, invoicing.
  2. Combine their published include: or ip4: mechanisms into a single SPF record.
  3. End the record with -all (hard fail). A trailing ~all only marks spoofed mail; -all rejects it.
  4. Publish it as one TXT record on the root domain.
  5. Keep it under the 10-DNS-lookup limit; flatten or consolidate includes if you exceed it.
Example record v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com include:sendgrid.net -all
Closes SPO-AU-01 SPO-HD-01

DKIM

DomainKeys Identified Mail

+25 pts

Adds a cryptographic signature receivers can verify — proof the message genuinely came from you and was not altered in transit.

Remediation
  1. Enable DKIM signing in each sending platform's admin console.
  2. Publish the public key it issues — usually a CNAME or TXT record at selector._domainkey.
  3. Use a 2048-bit key where the provider supports it.
  4. Make sure every sending service signs, not only your primary mailbox.
  5. Rotate keys periodically and retire old selectors.
Example record selector1._domainkey TXT v=DKIM1; k=rsa; p=MIIBIjANBgkq…
Closes SPO-AU-02 SPO-BR-01

DMARC

Domain-based Message Authentication

+25 pts

Ties SPF and DKIM to the visible From address and tells receivers what to do on failure — the only control that actually stops exact-domain spoofing.

Remediation
  1. Publish a TXT record at _dmarc with p=none and an rua= address to begin collecting reports.
  2. Read the aggregate reports and identify every legitimate source; fix the SPF or DKIM alignment of any that fail.
  3. Once your reports are clean, raise the policy to p=quarantine — optionally ramping with pct=.
  4. Finally raise it to p=reject for full enforcement.
  5. Keep monitoring reports for regressions and new senders.
Example record _dmarc TXT v=DMARC1; p=reject; rua=mailto:dmarc@yourdomain; adkim=s; aspf=s
Closes SPO-AU-03 SPO-HD-02 BEC-AC-04

MX

Mail Exchange

+25 pts

Directs inbound mail to servers that can receive and filter it. No MX, no mail — and no place to enforce inbound protection.

Remediation
  1. Point MX records at your mail provider's hosts with the correct priorities.
  2. Confirm the servers actually accept mail, not merely resolve in DNS.
  3. Remove stale MX entries left over from retired providers.
  4. Consider an inbound filtering gateway ahead of the mailbox to apply these same defensive checks.
Example record @ MX 1 aspmx.l.google.com

The rollout

The safe path to enforcement.

DMARC is powerful enough to drop your own mail if you rush it. This is the sequence that gets you to p=reject without collateral damage.

01
Publish SPF
Enumerate senders, build one record, end in -all.
02
Enable DKIM
Turn on signing everywhere and publish the keys.
03
DMARC p=none
Start monitoring; collect aggregate reports.
04
Align sources
Fix every legitimate sender that fails alignment.
05
p=quarantine
Enforce softly; ramp with pct if desired.
06
p=reject
Full enforcement — impersonation is dropped.

Move one step at a time, and let the reports guide you. Every change is a DNS edit you can verify in minutes with the domain check — and understand in depth through the spoofing pillar.
