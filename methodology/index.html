Methodology & Limitations v1.0

How every grade
is produced.

A reference is only as trustworthy as its method. This is the exact specification behind every SpamCrackers grade — what is measured, what is not, and where the uncertainty lives. Nothing here is aspirational; it describes the running system, and it is versioned so its meaning cannot silently drift.

01 · Data acquisition

Read directly from public DNS.

Every grade is derived only from public DNS records, queried over DNS-over-HTTPS from Google's resolver (dns.google/resolve). There is no crawling, no mail is sent, and nothing about the domain or the visitor is stored. Four record lookups are made:

SPF
TXT at the apex; the record beginning v=spf1 is read for its all qualifier.
DMARC
TXT at _dmarc.<domain>; the p= policy tag is read from the v=DMARC1 record.
MX
MX at the apex; presence of one or more hosts is scored.
DKIM
TXT at <selector>._domainkey.<domain> across 19 common selectors: google, selector1, selector2, default, s1, s2, k1, dkim, mail, mandrill, mxvault, pic, smtp, zoho, sig1, key1, fm1, fm2, fm3. The first key found scores DKIM.
02 · Scoring

Four controls, twenty-five points each.

The full domain check scores four controls out of 25 for a total out of 100. The mapping from record to points is fixed:

ControlConditionPoints
SPF-all hard fail25
SPF~all soft fail18
SPFneutral / permissive qualifier (?all, +all, no all)10
SPFno v=spf1 record0
DKIMkey found on a common selector25
DKIMno key on any queried selector0
DMARCp=reject25
DMARCp=quarantine20
DMARCp=none (record present, monitoring only)10
DMARCno v=DMARC1 record0
MXone or more MX hosts present25
MXno MX record0

04 · The two scores, reconciled

Why the Observatory number differs.

The Observatory uses a faster pass that grades only the three controls readable exactly from DNS — SPF, DMARC and MX — omitting DKIM, then rescales the 75 available points to 100: round(points ÷ 75 × 100).

They are not directly comparable. A domain with perfect SPF, DMARC and MX but no discoverable DKIM scores 100 (A+) in the Observatory, yet 75 (B) in the full check. The Observatory number is a DKIM-agnostic index of the three exact controls; the full check is the complete grade. Each page states which it shows, and both share the same grade bands.

This is a deliberate design choice: the Observatory measures many domains at scale without the DKIM selector guesswork that could produce false negatives, so its index stays exact and reproducible. The full check accepts the one bounded DKIM heuristic in exchange for completeness on a single domain.

05 · Scope

What is deliberately out of scope.

A defensible grade is precise about its boundaries. The following are not part of the score — stated plainly so no grade is read as more than it is.

DKIM completeness
DKIM is probed against 19 common selectors. A domain that signs on a custom selector can show DKIM as absent even when present — a false negative bounded to a single control. SPF, DMARC and MX are read directly and are exact.
Key strength & rotation
The presence of a DKIM key is scored, not its algorithm, bit length, or rotation hygiene.
SPF evaluation depth
The SPF qualifier is read from the published record. The 10-DNS-lookup limit, recursive include: resolution, and macro expansion are not evaluated.
DMARC enforcement in practice
The declared policy is read. Whether a given receiver honours it — and any pct, sp, or alignment-mode nuance — is out of scope.
Adjacent standards
BIMI, MTA-STS, TLS-RPT, ARC and DNSSEC are not part of the grade. They are complementary, not authentication of the From address.
Subdomains & sending sources
The grade describes the queried apex (or the exact host given). It does not enumerate subdomains, nor inspect actual outbound mail, content, or deliverability.
Non-sending domains
The MX control rewards a working mail path, so a domain that correctly sends and receives nothing — hardened with v=spf1 -all, DMARC p=reject and a null MX — scores no MX points and cannot reach the top grade, even though it is maximally spoof-resistant. The grade measures mail capability and posture together, not spoof-resistance alone; the web-only lockdown is the correct configuration for such a domain.
06 · Threats to validity

Where the uncertainty lives.

Every measurement carries assumptions. These are the ones that bound a SpamCrackers grade — named so they can be reasoned about rather than discovered.

Point-in-time
A grade reflects DNS as observed at query time. Records change; a grade is a snapshot, and the Observatory dates every measurement for exactly this reason.
Caching & propagation
Answers are subject to TTL caching and propagation delay. A change published moments earlier may not yet be globally visible.
Single resolver
All lookups use one DNS-over-HTTPS resolver (Google, dns.google/resolve). Split-horizon or geo-targeted DNS could return different records to a different resolver or region.
Declared vs. enforced
SPF and DMARC scores measure policy declaration. They are a strong proxy for protection, not a guarantee of any individual receiver's action.
Forwarding & ARC
Legitimate forwarding can break SPF and DKIM alignment downstream. The grade scores the sender's published posture, not survival across every forwarding path.
Integrity over completeness
If any domain in a scheduled Observatory run fails to resolve, the run aborts rather than publish a partial record — no measurement is preferred to a misleading one.
07 · Reproducibility & change control

Deterministic, and versioned.

Given the same DNS state, the method returns the same grade — there is no hidden model and no randomness. The rubric is published here in full; the Observatory's underlying data is open as latest.json under CC BY 4.0, re-measured weekly with an immutable archive, so results can be independently reproduced and audited against git history.

This methodology is v1.0. Grade semantics are stable: the bands and control weights above will not change meaning without a version bump recorded in governance. When the DKIM selector set or any weight changes, it is a documented, versioned change — never a silent one.
